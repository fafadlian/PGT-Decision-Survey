PGT Decision Survey: Results Report

Two-Level Analysis: General & Faculty-Specific Insights

How to Read This Report

This report presents findings from the PGT Decision Survey, which examines the factors associated with prospective students' decisions to accept or decline postgraduate taught (PGT) offers at the University of Sheffield.

Key Metrics

Metric Definition Interpretation Guide
Rate Difference (pp) Change in acceptance rate when a factor is present +10pp = 10 percentage points higher acceptance rate among respondents with that factor
Net Importance (% of acceptors citing factor) minus (% of decliners citing factor) Positive values indicate the factor is cited more frequently by acceptors; negative values indicate it is cited more frequently by decliners
Cramér's V Effect size measuring practical significance 0.10–0.20 = Small; 0.20–0.30 = Medium; >0.30 = Large
Confidence Interval Range within which the true population value is likely to fall Wider intervals indicate greater uncertainty

Statistical Notes

  • FDR-corrected results control for multiple testing across the full set of comparisons
  • Faculty-level findings are exploratory due to smaller sample sizes and use raw (uncorrected) p-values
  • Emphasis is placed on effect sizes and direction alongside statistical significance

Understanding Statistical Significance

A result is statistically significant when the observed difference between groups (e.g., acceptors vs decliners) is large enough that it is unlikely to have occurred by chance alone. In this report, we typically use a threshold of p < 0.05, meaning there is less than a 5% probability the result is due to random variation.

In plain terms: if a test is statistically significant, there is good evidence that a real difference exists between the groups — it is not just noise in the data.

Why "Popular" Does Not Mean "Differentiating"

A common misconception is that a factor cited frequently must be an important driver. This is not necessarily the case. What matters is whether the factor is cited differently by acceptors and decliners.

Scenario Example Interpretation
High for acceptors, low for decliners Course content cited by 55% of acceptors but only 28% of decliners This factor differentiates the two groups — it is associated with acceptance
Low for acceptors, high for decliners League tables cited by 10% of acceptors but 20% of decliners This factor differentiates in the opposite direction — it is associated with declining
High for both groups University reputation cited by 45% of acceptors and 42% of decliners This factor is popular but not differentiating — both groups value it equally, so it does not help explain why someone accepts or declines
Low for both groups Student forums cited by 5% of acceptors and 4% of decliners This factor is uncommon and not differentiating — it plays a minor role for everyone

Key insight: The most actionable findings are those where there is a large gap between the acceptor and decliner citation rates. A factor that is universally important (or universally unimportant) does not distinguish the two groups.

Effect Size: How Big Is the Difference?

Statistical significance tells us whether a difference exists, but effect size tells us how large that difference is. A test can be statistically significant but practically trivial if the sample is large enough. This report uses Cramér's V to measure effect size:

Cramér's V Interpretation Practical Implication
< 0.10 Negligible Difference exists but is too small to act on
0.10 – 0.20 Small Noticeable pattern; worth monitoring
0.20 – 0.30 Medium Meaningful difference; consider for strategy
> 0.30 Large Strong differentiator; high priority for action

Multiple Comparisons and FDR Correction

When many statistical tests are run simultaneously, some will appear significant purely by chance (false positives). For example, if 50 tests are run at p < 0.05, approximately 2–3 will appear significant even if no real effects exist.

To address this, the main university-wide analyses in this report apply FDR (False Discovery Rate) correction using the Benjamini–Hochberg method. This adjusts the p-values to control the expected proportion of false positives among significant results.

  • FDR-corrected results (§1.1) are the most reliable — they survived multiple-comparison adjustment
  • Uncorrected results (§2.3, faculty-level) are exploratory and should be treated as hypotheses for further investigation

Correlation vs Causation

All findings in this report are observational associations, not causal effects. For example, the finding that event attendance is associated with higher acceptance does not prove that attending an event causes someone to accept. It may be that applicants who were already likely to accept are also more likely to attend events. Alternative explanations should always be considered.

Level 1: General Analysis (University-Wide)

These findings are based on the full set of respondents regardless of faculty.

1.1 Key Drivers of Acceptance

Research Question

Which factors are statistically associated with the decision to accept or decline an offer at the university level?

Methodology

  • Test: Chi-square test of independence for each binary factor against acceptance/decline outcome
  • Purpose: To identify which individual factors show a statistically significant association with the decision to accept or decline
  • Multiple-comparison correction: Benjamini–Hochberg False Discovery Rate (FDR) applied across all comparisons to control for inflated Type I error
  • Effect size: Cramér's V, measuring the strength of association between each factor and the outcome

Parameters Measured

  • Outcome variable: Acceptance decision (Acceptor vs Decliner)
  • Predictor variables: Binary indicators for each survey factor (event attendance, information source usage, demographic groups, etc.)
  • Key metrics reported: Rate difference (percentage points), Cramér's V, FDR-corrected significance

Findings

The charts above show factors that reached statistical significance after FDR correction. Green bars indicate factors associated with higher acceptance rates; red bars indicate factors associated with lower acceptance rates.

Factors Associated with Higher Acceptance

Factor Rate Difference Detail
In-person event attendance +11pp Respondents who reported attending a campus event had an acceptance rate 11 percentage points higher than those who did not
Over 30 age group Positive Older respondents showed a higher acceptance rate
Email communication engagement Positive Respondents who engaged with email communications showed a higher acceptance rate

Factors Associated with Lower Acceptance

Factor Rate Difference Detail
Preference not to attend events −15pp Respondents who indicated a preference not to attend events had a 15pp lower acceptance rate
Comparison site usage Negative Respondents who reported using comparison sites showed a lower acceptance rate

Caveats

  • These findings are observational associations and do not establish causation
  • Respondents with stronger pre-existing intent may be more likely both to attend events and to accept an offer; the event attendance itself may not be the causal factor
  • All comparisons survived FDR correction, but effect sizes should be prioritised for practical interpretation

Summary

In-person event attendance and the over-30 age group show the strongest positive associations with acceptance. Comparison site usage and preference not to attend events show the strongest negative associations. These are statistical associations only; causal interpretation is not supported by this design.

Findings

12 of the 14 factors tested show statistically significant variation across the four segments. The heatmap reveals both consistent and segment-specific patterns.

Scholarships and Bursaries — largest segment effect (V = 0.288)

  • Overseas respondents cite this factor at much higher rates (~39%) regardless of whether they accepted or declined
  • Home respondents cite it infrequently (~10%)
  • The variation is primarily a home-versus-overseas distinction rather than an accept-versus-decline one

Course Content and Module Choices — home acceptors distinct

  • Home acceptors cite course content (63.9%) and module choices (45.4%) at notably higher rates than other segments
  • Overseas acceptors also cite these factors, but at lower rates
  • Both decliner groups cite them at approximately 25–30% and ~16% respectively

Location — more pronounced among overseas decliners

  • Home decliners (27.8%) and home acceptors (26.2%) cite location at similar rates
  • Among overseas respondents, decliners (21.9%) cite location at more than twice the rate of acceptors (9.6%), a 12pp gap

League Tables — cited more frequently by decliners in both segments

  • Home decliners (22.2%) and overseas decliners (15.1%) cite league tables at higher rates than their respective acceptor groups (16.4% and 6.0%)

Tuition Fees — no significant segment variation (p = 0.35)

  • Citation rates are broadly similar (17–25%) across all four segments

Cost of Living — consistent acceptor-versus-decliner pattern

  • Approximately 36–38% of acceptors cite this factor regardless of fee status
  • Only 11–14% of decliners cite it

Caveats

  • The chi-square test assumes independence of observations; respondents could cite multiple factors, which may introduce dependencies
  • Some decliner cells are small (Home decliners n = 36), increasing uncertainty in those estimates
  • Statistical significance does not imply practical importance; Cramér's V should be used to assess effect size

Summary

The most notable segment-specific pattern relates to scholarships and bursaries, which is predominantly an overseas concern. Most other factors divide primarily along accept/decline lines, with the exceptions of location (a stronger differentiator within the overseas segment) and course content/module choices (particularly associated with home acceptors). Cost of living is a consistent acceptor marker regardless of fee status.

2.4 Faculty-Level Driver Profiles

Research Question

What are the top drivers of acceptance within each individual faculty, and how do these differ from the university-wide pattern?

Methodology

  • Chi-square tests: Per-faculty chi-square test of independence for each binary variable against acceptance/decline. Variables excluded where either cell count falls below 5 or where the variable has no variation within the faculty.
  • Ordinal tests: Per-faculty Mann-Whitney U test for ordinal and continuous variables (NPS score, number of other universities considered, module importance, scholarship importance, funding understanding).
  • Effect size: Cramér's V for binary variables; rank-biserial r for ordinal variables.
  • Note: Raw (uncorrected) p-values are used as the primary threshold (p < 0.10) due to small within-faculty samples. FDR correction is also reported but few variables survive it at this granularity.

Parameters Measured

  • Outcome variable: Acceptance decision (Acceptor vs Decliner) within each faculty
  • Predictor variables: Binary indicators + ordinal measures
  • Key metrics reported: Rate difference (pp), effect size, raw p-value
  • Charts: One chart per faculty showing top positive and negative drivers; diverging bar charts where green = associated with higher acceptance, red = associated with lower acceptance

Cross-Faculty Patterns

  • In-person group events show positive associations with acceptance in Arts & Humanities (+27pp), Engineering (+20pp), and Social Sciences (+11pp) — consistent with the university-wide finding in §1.1
  • Number of universities applied to is a negative predictor across all five faculties, suggesting that applicants with more alternatives are consistently harder to convert

Faculty-Specific Patterns

  • Engineering stands out with strong negative associations for online events and scholarship importance — decliners in this faculty appear more price-sensitive and less engaged by online formats
  • Science is unique in showing university course web pages as a negative driver (−24pp); this may reflect comparison behaviour rather than the pages themselves being off-putting
  • Health shows a location-adjacent pattern: comparison website users and online-event attendees show lower acceptance, while personal growth motivation is a positive marker
  • Social Sciences — the largest faculty — shows the most significant results, with university communications (events, emails) as positive drivers and league table usage as a negative driver

  • All findings are observational and should not be interpreted as causal

Limitations

Limitation Implication
Observational data All findings are associations, not established causal effects. Confounding variables may explain observed patterns.
Self-selected sample Survey respondents may differ systematically from non-respondents, introducing potential response bias.
Small faculty-level samples Faculty-level findings are exploratory only and should be used for hypothesis generation.
Point-in-time snapshot Findings reflect this particular cohort and time period. Results may not generalise to future cohorts.
Correlation ≠ Causation For example, high-intent applicants may both attend events and accept offers — event attendance may not itself cause acceptance.
Survey routing Some questions (barriers, accept/decline factors) were presented only to specific subgroups. Comparisons across groups must account for this design.
